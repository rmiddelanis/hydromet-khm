\section{Introduction}
Cambodia aims to develop into a high-income country by 2050 from its current
status of a lower-middle income country as per the World Bank's country income
classification and the country. Extreme weather events pose a threat to this
development path by impacting key sectors of the economy, with floods being the
most frequent and damaging disasters, historically
\parencite{undrr2019disaster_risk_cambodia}. Ongoing climate change further
exacerbates these risks along Cambodia's development path, both through gradual
changes in temperature and precipitation patterns as well as through an increase
in the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events. 

One sector that is particularly vulnerable to changing precipitation patterns
and temperature is the agriculture sector
\parencite{wbadb2021climate_risk_cambodia}, which still accounts for 17\% of
Cambodia's GDP \parencite{wb2025agri_gdp_cambodia}. The country's electricity
generation relies heavily on hydropower, which accounted for 35\% of generated
electricity in 2023 \parencite{iea2023energy_mix_cambodia}. With altered
rainfall and extreme weather events, streamflow patterns can be expected to
change, affecting the country's electricity supply. As weather extremes become
more frequent and intense, damages to assets and infrastructure are likely to
increase, leading to higher economic losses from disasters. Under a pessimistic 
scenario, annual average flood damages in Cambodia could reach up to 2.97\% of
the national capital stock by 2050, compared to current levels of 0.93\%
\parencite{worldbank2023ccdr}.

Better weather forecasts can help mitigate these negative impacts by providing
timely information to farmers, energy producers, and policymakers. In the
agriculture sector, accurate forecasts can inform decisions about planting and
harvesting crops \parencite{an-vo_framework_2021}, while energy producers can
use forecasts to optimize their operations \parencite{koh_evaluating_2024}. They
also enable timely early warnings that allow for proactive measures to protect
infrastructure and communities, effectively reducing their impact on the economy
and society \parencite{middelanis2025warning}. 

Here we use the World Bank's macro-economic model \textit{MFMod}
\parencite{burns_world_2019} to estimate the economic benefits of improved
hydrometeorological forecasts in Cambodia. We focus on three impact channels:
(i) disaster risk reduction for reduced flood damages, (ii)
improved agricultural productivity, and (iii) optimized hydropower operation.
This technical background paper describes the data and methods used in the
analysis, presents the results, and discusses their implications for Cambodia's
development path.

\section{Scenarios}
\label{sec:scenarios}

We simulate the economic development of Cambodia under optimistic and
pessimistic climate projections (\cref{fig:temp_projections_khm}). We use a 10-y
running average of temperature projections from the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 CMIP6
ensembles, respectively, which we obtain from the World Bank's Climate Change
Knowledge Portal \parencite{worldbank2025climate_temp_cambodia}. The impact
channel variables used in the simulations are defined along the temperature
change under both scenarios relative to the 2020 base year. Under the optimistic
projection, the temperature in Cambodia will increase by about 0.6\degree~C
between 2020 and 2050, and by about 1.2\degree~C under the pessimistic
projection. {\color{red} Note: We could also use the lower and upper ensemble
deciles for the two scenarios, which would yield increases of 0.52\degree~C and
1.62\degree~C, respectively. This would align better with the DRR asset loss
estimates where the 20th and 80th percentiles are used, and we can use any
increases for the agriculture damage functions, but hydropower may be
problematic.} Under both climate scenarios, we simulate three forecast
scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Control (no forecasts)}: This control scenario models the development of
    Cambodia in the absence of hydrometeorological forecast benefits.
  \item \textbf{Maintaining the status quo}: This scenario represents the current
    forecast skill and its benefits for Cambodia. As the scenario represents the 
    current state, no additional investments are considered.
  \item \textbf{Continued improvement}: This scenario models an investment into
  hydrometeorological observations and institutional capacity to improve forecasts
  and early warning systems. The investment requires continued investments until
  the year 2050.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
In their 2013 report, \cite{WorldBank2013CambodiaHydroMet} assessed the state of
Cambodia's national meteorological and hydrological service capacity. They
identified significant gaps in comparison to other countries in the region, and
recommended investments to improve observation networks, forecasting systems,
and early warning systems. Investment needs were estimated at
approximately USD~16.1~million over a 5-year period\footnote{Costs were
estimated at up to USD~20.7~million in case of "stand alone" option without
regional cooperation.}. More recently, the World Meteorological Organization
(WMO) led an initiative to strengthen Cambodia's and Lao PDR's meteorological
and hydrological services. Investments were approximately USD~5.5~million in
both countries over a 4-year period from 2020 to 2024
\parencite{preventionweb2025lao_cambodia_project}, and an additional USD~7.8~million
is planned for the coming four years \parencite{crews2025sea2_project}. For our
simulations, we assume that the initially higher investment allowed Cambodia to reach
the current forecast level comparable to other countries in the region, and that
continued investments of USD~1~million per year (net present value) are necessary to further improve
Cambodia's hydrometeorological forecasts and early warning capacity to follow
the "perfect forecast skill" path. {\color{red}This corresponds to investments
equal to 0.02\% of Cambodia's 2024 GDP of USD~46.3~billion.}

In the following sections, we define variables for each impact channel under the
different climate and forecast scenarios for the simulation period from 2020 to
2050. These variables are used as inputs to the \textit{MFMod} model to simulate
Cambodia's economic development under the different scenarios.


\section{Disaster Risk Reduction}
\label{sec:drr}

Flood damages are expected to increase in Cambodia due to climate change and can
reach up to 2.97\% annual average loss of national capital by 2050 under a
pessimistic climate scenario, and up to 1.01\% under an optimistic
scenario\footnote{For these estimates, the optimistic and pessimistic climate
scenarios are defined slightly differently from ours, and are based on the 20th
and 80th percentiles of losses under the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 CMIP6 ensembles,
respectively.} from a current level of 0.93\% \parencite{worldbank2023ccdr}.
These estimates of future damages account for population growth and adjusted
land-use planning, but do not consider other adaptation measures such as early
warning systems. Improved forecasts can help reduce flood damages by enabling
early warnings and proactive measures to protect assets, infrastructure, and
livelihoods. 

We assume that projected flood damages under the two climate scenarios
materialize proportionally to the temperature increase relative to 2020 in the
absence of forecasts, which yields the "no forecasts" scenario. To define the
asset damages under the "status quo" 
scenario, we use values for Cambodia from \textcite{middelanis2025warning},
who estimate that the current level of forecast skill, institutional capacity,
and early warning coverage allow to reduce flood damages by 3.2\%. For the
"continued improvement" scenario, we assume that coverage increases to 100\%,
and that forecast quality and institutional capacity increase such that average warning
lead times reach the level of high-income countries, which yields an
estimated damage reduction of 6.5\% by 2050.

Annual average flood damages as a fraction of national capital stock are shown
for all three forecast scenarios under the two climate scenarios in
\cref{tab:drr_impact_channel_KHM} and \cref{fig:drr_impact_channel_KHM} for the period from 2020 to 2050. 
With current forecast skill, damages in the optimistic climate scenario are
reduced from 1.01\% in 2050 to 0.98\%. With perfect forecast skill, damages
decrease further to 0.95\% in 2050. Under the pessimistic climate scenario,
damages in 2050 are reduced from 2.97\% to 2.88\% with current forecast skill,
and to 2.78\% with perfect forecast skill.

\section{Agricultural Productivity}
\label{sec:agriculture}
Various studies have shown the value of weather forecasts for agricultural
productivity. \cite{collins_evaluating_2024} simulate the value of weather
forecasts for irrigation for 11 locations scheduling in 7 countries and find
maize yields to increase by up to 11\% compared to a no-forecast scenario.
Forecasts can also guide agronomic decisions such as optimal planting dates and
fertilizer use \parencite{hansen_potential_2009} and inform planting decisions
to increase agricultural productivity \parencite{tarchiani_access_2021}.

To quantify the benefits of weather forecasts for agricultural productivity in
Cambodia, we use estimates from \cite{an-vo_framework_2021}, who simulate the
value of improved forecasts on rice yields in the Vietnamese Mekong River Delta
region under current and perfect forecasts. They use a price of \$220 per ton of
rice and valuate current forecast skills at \$135\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} and
perfect forecast skills at \$220\,ha\textsuperscript{-1}. Assuming a rice yield
of 5.8 t\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} \parencite{stuart2018farm}, the value of current forecasts
translates to an increase in productivity of 10.34\%, and the value of perfect
forecasts to an increase of 16.86\%. If costs, inputs, and prices remain
constant, these productivity increases can be used as percentage increases of
total factor productivity (TFP). We apply these productivity increases to the
agriculture sector in Cambodia, which occupies a significant share of the work
force \parencite{WorldBank2013CambodiaHydroMet} and accounted for more than half
of total agricultural value added in the year 2023 according to estimates of the
Food and Agriculture Organization \parencite{fao_faostat_2025}.

In the absence of measures to improve agricultural productivity, climate change
is expected to reduce output in the agricultural sector. We calibrate these control
scenarios under the optimistic and pessimistic climate scenarios using
estimated damage functions from \cite{roson2016estimation} who find decreases in
agriculture multi-factor productivity of 2.53\% and 5.27\% under temperature increases
of 1\degree~C and 2\degree~C, respectively, for Cambodia. 

Annual agricultural productivity changes for all scenarios are shown in
\cref{tab:agri_impact_channel_KHM} and \cref{fig:agri_impact_channel_KHM} for
the period from 2020 to 2050. In the absence of forecasts, agricultural
productivity decreases by 1.52\% until 2050. This decrease can be offset if
current forecast skill is fully translated into productivity increases, leading
to a 8.82\% higher productivity in 2050 compared to the control scenario. With
perfect forecast skill, productivity would increase by up to 15.34\% in 2050.
Under the pessimistic climate scenario, productivity decreases by 3.64\% in 2050
in the control scenario, while it increases by 6.63\% and 13.15\% with
current and perfect forecast skill, respectively.
