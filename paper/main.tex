\section{Introduction}
Cambodia aims to develop into a high-income country by 2050 from its current
status of a lower-middle income country as per the World Bank's country income
classification and the country. Climate change poses challenges to this
development path, both through gradual changes in temperature and precipitation
patterns as well as through an increase in the frequency and intensity of
extreme weather events such as floods and droughts. These extreme weather events
can have significant negative impacts on various sectors of the economy. 
Historically, floods have been the most
frequent and damaging disasters in Cambodia \parencite{undrr2019disaster_risk_cambodia}. 
The agriculture sector accounts for 17\% of Cambodia's GDP 
\parencite{wb2025agri_gdp_cambodia}, and is particularly vulnerable to changing 
precipitation patterns and temperature \parencite{wbadb2021climate_risk_cambodia}.
The country's electricity generation relies heavily on hydropower,
which accounted for 35\% of generated electricity in 2023 \parencite{iea2023energy_mix_cambodia}.
With altered rainfall and extreme weather events, streamflow patterns can be expected
to change, affecting the country's electricity supply.

Better weather forecasts can help mitigate these negative impacts by providing
timely information to farmers, energy producers, and policymakers. Accurate
forecasts can help farmers make informed decisions about planting and harvesting
crops \parencite{an-vo_framework_2021}, while energy producers can use forecasts
to optimize their operations \parencite{koh_evaluating_2024}. They also enable
timely early warnings that allow for proactive measures to protect
infrastructure and communities, effectively reducing their impact on the economy
and society \parencite{middelanis2025warning}. Here we use the World Bank's
macro-economic model \textit{MFMod} \parencite{burns_world_2019} to estimate the
economic benefits of improved hydrometeorological forecasts in Cambodia. We
focus on three impact channels: (i) disaster risk reduction, focusing on reduced
damage from floods, (ii) improved agricultural productivity, and (iii) optimized
hydropower generation. This technical background paper describes the data and
methods used in the analysis, presents the results, and discusses their
implications for Cambodia's development path.

\section{Scenarios}
\label{sec:scenarios}

We simulate the economic development of Cambodia under optimistic and
pessimistic climate projections (\cref{fig:temp_projections_khm}). We use a 10-y
running average of temperature projections from the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 CMIP6
ensembles, respectively, which we obtain from the World Bank's Climate Change
Knowledge Portal \parencite{worldbank2025climate_temp_cambodia}. For the
definition of impact channel variables in the simulations, we use the
temperature change under both scenarios relative to the 2020 base year. Under
the optimistic projection, the temperature in Cambodia will increase by about
0.6\degree~C between 2020 and 2050, and by about 1.2\degree~C under the
pessimistic projection. {\color{red} Note: We could also use the lower and upper
ensemble deciles for the two scenarios, which would yield increases of
0.52\degree~C and 1.62\degree~C, respectively. This would align better with the
DRR asset loss estimates where the 20th and 80th percentiles are used, and we can use any increases for the agriculture
damage functions, but hydropower may be problematic.} Under both climate
scenarios, we consider three forecast scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No forecasts}: This control scenario models the development of
    Cambodia in the absence of hydrometeorological forecasts. It is used to
    quantify the benefits of forecasts by comparing it to the other two forecast
    scenarios.
  \item \textbf{Current forecast skill}: This scenario represents the current
    forecast skill and its benefits for Cambodia. 
  \item \textbf{Perfect forecast skill}: This scenario models an investment into
  hydrometeorological observations and institutional capacity to improve forecasts
  and early warning systems. The investment is spread over a 10-year period from
  2031 to 2040, after which the improved forecast skill and associated benefits 
  are maintained until 2050.
\end{itemize}

\section{Disaster Risk Reduction}
\label{sec:drr}

Flood damages are expected to increase in Cambodia due to climate change and can
reach up to 2.97\% annual average loss of national capital by 2050 under a
pessimistic climate scenario, and up to 1.01\% under an optimistic
scenario\footnote{For these estimates, the optimistic and pessimistic climate
scenarios are defined slightly differently from ours, and are based on the 20th
and 80th percentiles of losses under the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 CMIP6 ensembles,
respectively.} from a current level of 0.93\% \parencite{worldbank2023ccdr}.
These estimates of future damages account for population growth and adjusted
land-use planning, but do not consider other adaptation measures such as early
warning systems. Improved forecasts can help reduce flood damages by enabling
early warnings and proactive measures to protect assets, infrastructure, and
livelihoods. 

We assume that projected flood damages under the two climate scenarios
materialize proportionally to the temperature increase relative to 2020 in the
absence of forecasts, which yields the "no forecasts" scenario. To define the
asset damages under the "current forecast skill" and "perfect forecast skill"
scenarios, we use values for Cambodia from \textcite{middelanis2025warning},
who estimate that the current level of forecast skill, institutional capacity,
and early warning coverage allow to reduce flood damages by 3.2\%. For the 
"perfect forecast skill" scenario, we assume 100\% coverage, and
that forecast quality and institutional capacity increase such that average warning
lead times reach the level of high-income countries, which yields an
estimated damage reduction of 6.5\%. The improvement to perfect forecast skill is
assumed to take place linearly over the investment period from 2031 to 2040.

Annual average flood damages as percentage of national capital stock are shown
for all three forecast scenarios under the two climate scenarios in
\cref{tab:drr_impact_channel_KHM} and \cref{fig:drr_impact_channel_KHM} for the period from 2020 to 2050. 
With current forecast skill, damages in the optimistic climate scenario are
reduced from 1.01\% in 2050 to 0.98\%. With perfect forecast skill, damages
decrease further to 0.95\% in 2050. Under the pessimistic climate scenario,
damages in 2050 are reduced from 2.97\% to 2.88\% with current forecast skill,
and to 2.78\% with perfect forecast skill.

\section{Agricultural Productivity}
\label{sec:agriculture}
Various studies have shown the value of weather forecasts for agricultural
productivity. \cite{collins_evaluating_2024} simulate the value of weather
forecasts for irrigation for 11 locations scheduling in 7 countries and find
maize yields to increase by up to 11\% compared to a no-forecast scenario.
Forecasts can also guide agronomic decisions such as optimal planting dates and
fertilizer use. Simulations of forecast value for maize planting in Kenya
\parencite{hansen_potential_2009} and a case study in Burkina Faso
\parencite{tarchiani_access_2021} demonstrate that tailoring planting and input
choices to weather forecasts can increase agricultural productivity.

To quantify the benefits of weather forecasts for agricultural productivity in
Cambodia, we use estimates from \cite{an-vo_framework_2021}, who simulate the
value of improved forecasts on rice yields in the Vietnamese Mekong River Delta
region under current and perfect forecasts. They use a price of \$220 per ton of
rice and valuate current forecast skills at \$135\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} and
perfect forecast skills at \$220\,ha\textsuperscript{-1}. Assuming a rice yield
of 5.8 tons/ha \parencite{stuart2018farm}, the value of current forecasts
translates to an increase in productivity of 10.34\%, and the value of perfect
forecasts to an increase of 16.86\%. If costs, inputs, and prices remain
constant, these productivity increases can be used as percentage increases
of total factor productivity (TFP). We apply these productivity increases to
the agriculture sector in Cambodia, which accounted for more than half of total
agricultural output in the year 2023, according to estimates by the Food and
Agriculture Organization \parencite{fao_faostat_2025}.

In the absence of measures to improve agricultural productivity, climate change
is expected to reduce output in the agricultural sector. We calibrate these "no
forecast" baselines under the optimistic and pessimistic climate scenarios using
estimated damage functions from \cite{roson2016estimation} who find decreases in
agriculture multi-factor productivity of 2.53\% and 5.27\% under temperature increases
of 1\degree~C and 2\degree~C, respectively, for Cambodia. 

Annual agricultural productivity increases under the three forecast scenarios
and the two climate scenarios are shown in \cref{tab:agri_impact_channel_KHM}
and \cref{fig:agri_impact_channel_KHM} for the period from 2020 to 2050. In the
absence of forecasts, agricultural productivity decreases by 1.52\% until 2050.
This trend can be offset if current forecast skill is fully translated into
productivity increases, leading to a 8.82\% higher productivity in 2050. With perfect
forecast skill, productivity would increase by up to 15.34\% in 2050. Under the
pessimistic climate scenario, productivity decreases by 3.64\% in 2050 in the
"no forecasts" scenario, while it increases by 6.63\% and 13.15\% with current
and perfect forecast skill, respectively.
