\begin{abstract}
  \noindent
  This paper estimates the macroeconomic benefits of improved
  hydrometeorological forecast services in Cambodia using the World Bank's MFMod
  macro-fiscal model. Three impact channels---disaster risk reduction (DRR) for
  flood damages, agricultural productivity increase, and electricity cost
  reduction---are considered under optimistic and pessimistic climate change
  projections until the year 2050. Benefits under current forecast skill and
  continued investment into forecast improvements are compared to a control
  scenario without any forecast benefits. When all channels operate jointly, GDP
  is estimated to be 2.50--4.30\% above the no-forecast control by 2050.
  Continued improvement of forecast services yields a 1.3--1.4\% increase in GDP, compared to the status quo scenario with constant
  forecast skill. Agricultural productivity is the dominant channel for economic
  benefits, while DRR contributions grow with climate severity and become
  increasingly important under the pessimistic scenario. Electricity cost
  reduction (through improved hydropower production) contributes positively
  across all scenarios, though at a lower order of magnitude. Aggregate benefits
  are larger under the pessimistic climate scenario, because DRR channel returns
  amplify with increasing flood exposure. Back-of-the-envelope estimates suggest
  large benefit-cost ratios, making an economic case for improved
  hydrometeorological forecasts and services. The results provide quantitative
  support for sustained investment in hydrometeorological infrastructure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cambodia aims to develop into a high-income country by 2050 from its current
status of a lower-middle income country as per the World Bank's country income
classification. Extreme weather events pose a threat to this development path by
impacting key sectors of the economy, with floods being the most frequent and
damaging disasters, historically \parencite{undrr2019disaster_risk_cambodia}.
Ongoing climate change further exacerbates these risks along Cambodia's
development path, both through gradual changes in temperature and precipitation
patterns as well as through an increase in the frequency and intensity of
extreme weather events. The impacts of gradual climate change and
intensification of extreme weather events can materialize in Cambodia's economy
through various channels. Here we focus on three of these impact channels, and
assess how improved hydrometeorological forecasts and services can reduce the
resulting risks for the country's economic development.

First, as weather extremes become more frequent and intense, flood damages to
assets and infrastructure are expected to increase, leading to higher economic
losses from disasters. Under a pessimistic scenario, annual average flood
damages in Cambodia could reach up to 2.97\% of the national capital stock by
2050, compared to current levels of 0.93\% \parencite{worldbank2023ccdr}.

Second, one sector that is particularly vulnerable to changing precipitation
patterns and temperature is the agriculture sector
\parencite{wbadb2021climate_risk_cambodia}, which still accounts for 17\% of
Cambodia's GDP \parencite{wb2025agri_gdp_cambodia}. Projected increases in
temperature and changes in rainfall patterns are expected to affect crop yields
and increase production volatility, threatening incomes and food security.
\textcite{roson2016estimation} estimate that agricultural productivity in
Cambodia could decline by up to 8.2\% in a 3\degree\,C warmer world, compared to
the 1980--2004 period. 

Third, Cambodia's electricity generation relies heavily on hydropower, which
accounted for 35\% of generated electricity in 2023
\parencite{iea2023energy_mix_cambodia}. With altered rainfall and extreme
weather events, streamflow patterns will change and likely affect the country's
electricity supply. Most studies for the region project overall intensified
river discharge, but these increases materialize mostly in the wet season. Dry
season discharge is expected to decrease
\parencite{inseeyong2025evaluation,horton2022cambodian,li2021assessment},
reducing reliability of energy generation and potentially constraining economic
activity through higher energy system costs. 


Better weather forecasts can help mitigate these negative impacts by providing
timely information to households, farmers, energy producers, and policymakers.
In the agriculture sector, accurate forecasts can increase production yields and
avoid losses, for instance by informing planting and harvesting decisionis
\parencite{an-vo_framework_2021}. At global scale, cereal agriculture benefits
of weather forecasts have been estimated at up to USD~231~billion
\parencite{farkas2025economic}. Weather forecasts can be used by energy
producers to optimize operations, for example by taking energy generation and
discharge control decisions based on weather forecasts
\parencite{koh_evaluating_2024}. For Cambodia's neighbor country Viet Nam,
\textcite{giuliani2015making} estimate that the hydropower benefits of weather
forecasts in Viet Nam could reach up to USD~1.9~million per year. Better
hydrometeorological forcasts and services also enable timely early warnings that
allow for proactive measures to protect infrastructure and communities,
effectively reducing their impact on the economy and society
\parencite{middelanis2025warning}. According to \textcite{hallegatte_cost_2012},
disaster-related annual asset damages in developing countries could be reduced
by USD~300~million to USD~3~billion if early warning systems are improved to
developed-country standards.

The economic benefits of better weather services are often assessed on a
sector-by-sector basis \parencite{farkas2025economic}. One strand of studies
uses sectoral simulation models to create counterfactual scenarios that
represent the improvement of weather observations and services, and test the
benefits of such scenarios to the system outcomes (e.g.,
\cite{koh_evaluating_2024,pappenberger2015monetary}). Another strand of studies
is more empirical in nature, for instance, by using historical household survey
to see the differences in outcomes of farmers with access to higher forecast
accuracy (e.g., \cite{amegnaglo2022use,dupdal2020farmers}). What is less
obvious and studied is how these sectoral benefits translate into economy-wide
benefits at a country level. Furthermore, such sectoral benefits are often
assessed in isolation, while an economy-wide assessment of the hydromet services
benefits through multiple impact channels is missing.

To fill this research gap, we assess the economy-wide integrated benefits of
hydromet forecasts and services using the World Bank's macro-economic model
\textit{MFMod} \parencite{burns_world_2019} to estimate the economic benefits of
improved hydrometeorological forecasts in Cambodia. We focus on three impact
channels: (i) \textbf{disaster risk reduction for reduced flood damages}, (ii)
\textbf{improved agricultural productivity}, and (iii) \textbf{optimized
hydropower operation}. This technical background paper describes the data and
methods used in the analysis, presents the results, and discusses their
implications for Cambodia's development path.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \input{figures/flow_diagram}
  \caption{\textbf{Conceptual setup of the study.}}
  \label{fig:setup_flow_diagram}
\end{figure}

\cref{fig:setup_flow_diagram} illustrates the overall structure of our analysis.
To assess the economy-wide benefits of hydromet services, we consider scenarios
with different forecast skill and climate change projections, which feed into
the impact channels of disaster risk, agriculture productivity, and hydropower
generation. Impact channels are then integrated within the economic model
(MFMod) to produce GDP projections, which are the main outcome variable of
interest in this study. In the following, we describe the setup of scenarios
(\cref{sec:methods_scenarios}) and impact channels
(\cref{sec:methods_impact_channels}), and give an overview of the used
macro-economic model (\cref{sec:methods_mfmod}).


\subsection{Scenarios}
\label{sec:methods_scenarios}

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=5in]{./figures/temperature_projections_KHM.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Temperature projections for Cambodia.} Projections are shown
    for the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 (ensemble median and 10th/90th percentile). Optimistic and pessimistic
    scenarios for the simulation period from 2020 to 2050 are given by the 10-y
    running central averages of the respective 10-th and 90-th percentiles of
    the CMIP6 ensemble. Vertical bars indicate projected temperature increases
    under the optimistic and pessimistic scenarios between the years 2020 and
    2050. Data obtained from \cite{worldbank2025climate_temp_cambodia}.}
  \label{fig:temp_projections_khm}
\end{figure}

We simulate the economic development of Cambodia under optimistic and
pessimistic climate projections (\cref{fig:temp_projections_khm}). We use an
optimistic (pessimistic) scenario as the 10-y running average of annual average
temperature projections from the 10-th (90-th) percentile of the SSP1-2.6
(SSP5-8.5) CMIP6 ensemble, which we obtain from the World Bank's Climate Change
Knowledge Portal \parencite{worldbank2025climate_temp_cambodia}. The impact
channel variables used in the simulations are defined along the change of annual
average temperatures under both scenarios relative to the 2020 base year. Under
the optimistic projection, the temperature in Cambodia will increase by
0.52\degree\,C between 2020 and 2050, and by 1.62\degree\,C under the pessimistic
projection. Under both climate scenarios, we simulate three forecast scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Control (no forecasts)}: This control scenario models the
    development of Cambodia with climate change but in the absence of
    hydrometeorological forecast benefits.
  \item \textbf{Maintaining the status quo}: This scenario represents the
    current forecast skill and its benefits for Cambodia. As the scenario
    represents the current forecast state, no investments into additional
    hydrometeorological infrastructure are considered, but we assume annual
    maintenance costs for existing infrastructure. Importantly, the status quo 
    scenario holds the forecast skill constant, but assumes some institutional 
    progress, as described for each impact channel below. 
  \item \textbf{Continued improvement}: This scenario models an investment into
  hydrometeorological observations and institutional capacity to improve
  forecasts, assuming additional investments until the year 2050. It is identical
  to the status quo scenario with regards to progress in institutional capacity, 
  but includes a constantly increasing forecast skill. 
\end{itemize}
Forecast skill itself will also likely be affected by climate change, e.g. due
to changes in weather patterns and increased uncertainty. Here, we do not
account for this relationship and assume that forecast skill is independent of
the climate scenarios.
 

\noindent
In their 2013 report, \cite{WorldBank2013CambodiaHydroMet} assessed the state of
Cambodia's national meteorological and hydrological service capacity. They
identified significant gaps in comparison to other countries in the region, and
recommended investments to improve observation networks, forecasting systems,
and early warning systems. Investment needs were estimated at approximately
USD~16.1~million over a 5-year period\footnote{Costs were estimated at up to
USD~20.7~million in case of "stand alone" option without regional cooperation.}.
More recently, the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) led an initiative to
strengthen Cambodia's and Lao PDR's meteorological and hydrological services.
Investments were approximately USD~5.5~million in both countries over a 4-year
period from 2020 to 2024 \parencite{preventionweb2025lao_cambodia_project}, and
an additional USD~7.8~million is planned for the coming four years
\parencite{crews2025sea2_project}. The initially higher investment has allowed
Cambodia to reach the current forecast level comparable to other countries in
the region \parencite{chd2024cambodia}. We assume that annual costs of
USD~0.5~million (net present value) are required for operation and maintenance
(OPEX in the status quo scenario), and that continued service improvement
requires an additional annual expenditure of USD~0.5~million (additional OPEX in
the continued improvement scenario), together amounting to 0.02\% of Cambodia's
2024 GDP of USD~46.3~billion. Hydrometerological equipment has a lifetime of
about 15 years, and we assume that USD~21~million CAPEX will be required over a
period between 2031 to 2035 to fund improved infrastructure in the continued
improvement scenario. This amount is estimated as the 2025 net present
value\footnote{Price adjustment is done using the US GDP deflator rates for the
years 2013 and 2025.} of the originally estimated USD~16~million investment for
2013, adjusted for inflation \parencite{us2025deflator}. We assume no future
CAPEX in the status quo scenario. For the control scenario, OPEX and CAPEX are
zero throughout the entire simulation time. Scenario costs are summarized in
\cref{tab:scenario_costs_KHM}.

\subsection{Impact channels}
\label{sec:methods_impact_channels}
In the following sections, we derive the three impact channel variables that
serve as inputs to the \textit{MFMod} macroeconomic model (described in
\cref{sec:methods_mfmod}). For each channel, we compute annual shock values
under all climate and forecast scenarios throughout the 2020--2050 simulation
period. These computed values are then passed to MFMod as sector-specific shocks
to simulate their economy-wide GDP effects.

\subsubsection{Disaster Risk Reduction}
\label{sec:methods_drr}

We consider flooding for the disaster risk reduction impact channel due to
Cambdia's high risk to floods, making it one of the most flood-exposed countries
in the world \parencite{wbadb2021climate_risk_cambodia}. To get flood damages
across climate scenarios, we leverage modelled estimates from
\textcite{worldbank2023ccdr} and apply the methodology described in
\textcite{middelanis2025warning} to estimate early warning benefits in terms of
flood damage reduction.

Flood damages are expected to increase in Cambodia due to climate change and can
reach up to 2.97\% annual average loss of national capital by 2050 under a
pessimistic climate scenario, and up to 1.01\% under an optimistic
scenario\footnote{For these estimates, the optimistic and pessimistic climate
scenarios are defined slightly differently from ours, and are based on the 20th
and 80th percentiles of losses under the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 CMIP6 ensembles,
respectively.} from a current level of 0.93\% \parencite{worldbank2023ccdr} (not
accounting for early warning systems). These estimates of future damages account
for population growth and adjusted land-use planning, but do not consider other
adaptation measures such as early warning systems. Improved forecasts can help
reduce flood damages by enabling early warnings and proactive measures to
protect assets, infrastructure, and livelihoods. 

We assume that projected flood damages under the two climate scenarios
materialize proportionally to the temperature increase relative to 2020 in the
absence of forecasts, which yields the "no forecasts" scenario. To estimate the
benefits of early warning systems to reduce asset damages, we use values for
Cambodia from \textcite{middelanis2025warning}, who estimate that current early
warning systems allow to reduce flood damages by 3.18\%, based on estimated
population coverage of early warning systems and average realized warning lead
times. This damage reduction $\pi$ is calculated as
\begin{equation}
  \pi = \epsilon_{WRN} \cdot \pi_{max} \cdot \left(1 - e^{-\lambda_{WRN}/\tau}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{WRN}=0.69$ is the fraction of the population covered,
$\pi_{max}=0.097$ is the fraction of assets that can be protected (estimated as
the fraction moveable assets in total capital stock), $\lambda_{WRN}=7$ is
the average warning lead time in hours, and $\tau=10.92$ a scaling parameter as
estimated by \textcite{middelanis2025warning}. Currently realized average early
warning lead times are set according to country income group, assuming average
flood warnings of 7~hrs in lower-middle income countries and 12~hrs for
high-income countries.

Coverage is mainly determined by local institutional capacities. This
includes the ability to interpret forecastas in terms of socio-economic risk,
and to subsequently generate warnings and broadly disseminate actionable
information among the population at risk. Therefore, it can be assumed
independent of forecast skill and we model a growth of population coverage under
both the status quo and improvement scenarios, increasing from currently 69\% to
100\% in 2050. Under the improvement scenario, we additionally assume an
increase in forecast quality that reduces average early warning lead times to
the level of high-income countries by 2050. This increases damage reduction
benefits from currently 3.18\% to 4.59\% in 2050 under the status quo scenario
(due to better coverage), and 6.47\% under the continued improvement scenario
(due to better coverage and forecasts). 

Annual average flood damages resulting from projected flood losses and early
warning benefits are shown for all three forecast scenarios under the two
climate scenarios in \cref{tab:drr_impact_channel_KHM} and
\cref{fig:drr_impact_channel_KHM} for the period from 2020 to 2050. With current
forecast skill, damages in the optimistic climate scenario are reduced from
1.01\% in 2050 to 0.97\%. If forecast skill can be improved, damages decrease
further to 0.95\% in 2050. Under the pessimistic climate scenario, damages in
2050 are reduced from 2.97\% to 2.84\% with current forecast skill, and to
2.78\% with improved forecasts. 

These damages enter the MFMod model as the additional damage due to floods every
year (variable $RD_t$ in \cref{eq:drr_link_equation}, see \cref{sec:methods_mfmod_drr}).

\subsubsection{Agricultural Productivity}
\label{sec:methods_agriculture}
For the agricultural impact channel, we model productivity increases from
weather forecasts for the agriculture sector in Cambodia, which occupies a
significant share of the work force \parencite{WorldBank2013CambodiaHydroMet}
and accounted for more than half of total agricultural value added in the year
2023 according to estimates of the Food and Agriculture Organization
\parencite{fao_faostat_2025}. Various studies have shown the value of weather
forecasts for agricultural productivity. \cite{collins_evaluating_2024} simulate
the value of weather forecasts for irrigation for 11 locations scheduling in 7
countries and find maize yields to increase by up to 11\% compared to a
no-forecast scenario. Forecasts can also guide agronomic decisions such as
optimal planting dates and fertilizer use \parencite{hansen_potential_2009} and
inform planting decisions to increase agricultural productivity
\parencite{tarchiani_access_2021}. 

Recent initiatives aim to increase the use of weather and climate services in
Cambodia's agriculture sector. For example, the De-Risk Southeast Asia
initiative, launched in 2018, promotes use and dissemination of agroclimatic
advisories in two Cambodian regions, using seasonal forecasts
\parencite{swaans2023agroadvisories}. The PEARL project, financed by the Green
Climate Fund (GCF), aims to increase climate resilience of agricultural
production in the Northern Tonle Sap Basin in Cambodia. It is under
implementation since 2023 and planned for completion in 2029. As these projects
were launched only recently, we assume no agricultural benefits from forecasts
in our simulation base year of 2020, but model a linear increase until 2050,
where we assume that 100\% of potential forecast benefits are attained.

To quantify the potential benefits of weather forecasts for agricultural
productivity in Cambodia, we use estimates from \cite{an-vo_framework_2021}, who
simulate the value of improved forecasts on rice yields in the Vietnamese Mekong
River Delta region under current and perfect forecasts. The study valuates
current forecast value at \$135\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} and perfect forecast
skills at \$220\,ha\textsuperscript{-1}, assuming a price of \$225 per ton of
rice. If these benefits materialize in the form of increased output, this
implies a production increase of 0.6\,t\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} and
0.978\,t\,ha\textsuperscript{-1}, respectively. With a rice yield of 5.8
t\,ha\textsuperscript{-1} \parencite{stuart2018farm}, the estimated value of
current forecasts translates to an increase in productivity of $10.34\%$ [$=(5.8
+ 0.6) / 5.8 - 1$], and the value of perfect forecasts to an increase of
$16.86\%$ [=$(5.8 + 0.978) / 5.8 - 1$]. If costs, inputs, and prices remain
constant, these productivity increases can be used as percentage increases of
total factor productivity (TFP). 

In the absence of measures to improve agricultural productivity, climate change
is expected to reduce output in the agricultural sector. We calibrate the
control scenarios under the optimistic and pessimistic climate scenarios using
damage functions from \cite{roson2016estimation} who find decreases in
agriculture multi-factor productivity of 2.53\%, 5.27\%, and 8.2\% under
temperature increases of 1\degree\,C, 2\degree\,C, and 3\degree\,C, respectively,
for Cambodia. These estimates are in reference to a baseline period from 1980 to
2004, and we first adjust them to reflect changes relative to the year 2020. For
the adjustment, we assume a temperature increase of 0.725\degree\,C between 1992
and 2020 based on the 10-year rolling mean of the CMIP6 ensemble
data.\footnote{The rolling mean for the SSP2-1.6 and SSP5-8.5 scenarios indicate
temperature increases of 0.72\degree\,C and 0.73\degree\,C between 1992 and 2020,
respectively.} Then, the adjusted productivity change for a given temperature
change $\Delta T$ after 2020 in \degree\,C is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta p^{>2020}\left(\Delta T\right) = \frac{1 + \Delta p^{>1992}\left(\Delta T + 0.725\right)}{1 + \Delta p^{>1992}\left(0.725\right)} - 1,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta p^{>1992}\left(\Delta T\right)$ is the productivity change
relative to 1992, for which we linearly interpolate between data points from
\cite{roson2016estimation}. 

Annual agricultural productivity changes for all scenarios are shown in
\cref{tab:agri_impact_channel_KHM} and \cref{fig:agri_impact_channel_KHM} for
the period from 2020 to 2050. In the absence of forecasts, agricultural
productivity decreases by 1.43\% in the optimistic, and 4.52\% in the
pessimistic scenario until 2050. This decrease can be offset if the status quo
forecast skill can be fully translated into productivity increases, leading to
an overall 8.77\% and 5.35\% higher productivity in 2050. With continually
improved forecast skill, productivity would increase by up to 15.19\% or 11.57\%
in 2050.

These changes in annual agricultural productivity enter the MFMod model as a
change in total factor productivity, which affects the potential economic output
(variable $AD_t$ in \cref{eq:agri_link_equation}, see \cref{sec:methods_mfmod_productivity}).

\subsubsection{Hydropower Generation}
\label{sec:methods_hydropower}
Cambodia's electricity generation relies heavily on hydropower, which accounted
for 35\% of generated electricity in 2023
\parencite{iea2023energy_mix_cambodia}. Hydropower reservoirs and dams are
currently operated on rule curves, setting target water levels that reservoirs
should reach at specific times throughout the calendar year. Recent simulations
by \cite{koh_evaluating_2024} suggest that forecast-based operations could
improve hydropower production, thus reducing reliance on fossil fuels and
overall electricity generation. We here use simulated electricity system
operation costs from \cite{koh_evaluating_2024} to estimate forecast benefits in
the electricity sector in terms of reduction in electricity generation cost.

The simulations were conducted using 40-day forecast ensembles of river
discharges, driving a coupled reservoir and power system model. The study also
reports benefits under a perfect forecast scenario. Using the study's simulation
output, we estimate the cost reduction potential that could be realized through
switching from purely rule-based operations to forecast-informed operations.
Forecast-informed operations are not applied in Cambodia as of now, and
therefore none of the potential benefits are currently realized. Accordingly,
current benefits are assumed to be zero in all scenarios. While the control
scenario remains at this level, we assume that the fraction of potential
benefits realized (which is independent of hydromet investments, but rather a
function of hydropower operations) grows linearly up to to 100\% in the year
2050 under the status quo and continued improvement scenarios. The potential
benefits can be leveraged through hydromet investments in the improvement
scenario and is assumed constant under status quo conditions.

To estimate current potential benefits under the status quo and improvement
scenarios, we map simulated benefits from using the forecast ensemble to the
status quo scenario, and the perfect forecast simulations the continued
improvement scenario. As \cite{koh_evaluating_2024} found that forecast benefits
vary beetween seasons, we calculate the potential benefits as the mean electricity cost
reduction that can be obtained when choosing the cost-optimal operation mode for
each month throughout the year. For the continued improvement scenario, the
benefits are calculated from
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{|Y|}\sum_{y\in Y} \left(1 - \frac{\sum_{m=1}^{12} \min{\left(\mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{RB}, \mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{FE}, \mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{PF}\right)}}{\sum_{m=1}^{12} \mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{RB}}\right) \cdot 100,
  \label{eq:hydropower_monthly_cost_reduction}
\end{equation}
where $Y$ are the simulated years from 2000 to 2018, and
$\mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{RB}$, $\mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{FE}$, and $\mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{PF}$
are the system operating costs during the $m$-th month in year $y$ under
rule-based operations (RB) or forecast-based operations using the ensemble mean
of forecasts (FC) or perfect forecasts (PF). Curent potential benefits under the
status quo scenario are obtained by omitting operation costs under the perfect
forecast skill $\mathcal{C}_{m,y}^{PF}$ from the minimum operator in
\cref{eq:hydropower_monthly_cost_reduction}. The above yields current potential
annual cost reduction of 7.7\%, and 9.1\% if forecast skill was improved.

As the climate continues to warm, precipitation patterns in Cambodia and the
region will change. Projections suggest overall wetter years under both the
SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 scenarios \parencite{wb2025ckp}. Accordingly, most studies
predict overall increases in river discharge
\parencite{inseeyong2025evaluation,ly2023integrated,hoang2016mekong}, but the
expected change is subject to large uncertainty \parencite{oeurng2019assessing}
due to substantial internal variability. Projected changes are seasonally heterogeneos
and river discharge will likely increase in the wet season, and decrease in the dry season
\parencite{inseeyong2025evaluation,horton2022cambodian,li2021assessment}. 

These changes in seasonal discharge patterns will likely affect the potential
for cost reduction as calculated above. To obtain estimates for cost reduction
benefits under climate change, we combine the results from
\cite{koh_evaluating_2024} with results from \cite{wang2024historical} who
investigated projected discharge for the Mekong River basin at various gauge
stations, including Stung Treng station in Cambodia. We map projected
discharge changes for this station under RCP2.6 and RCP8.5 to our
optimistic and pessimistic climate scenarios, respectively. Then, we calculate
the expected average seasonal discharge $\overline{q_s^{sc}}$ under scenario
conditions (optimistic or pessimistic) as
\begin{equation}
  \overline{q_s^{sc}} = \frac{1}{|Y| |M_s|} \sum_{y\in Y} \sum_{m\in M_s} \left(1 + \Delta q_m^{sc}\right) q_{m,y},
\end{equation}
where $q_{m, y}$ is the observed discharge during month $m$ in year $y$ used by
\cite{koh_evaluating_2024}, $\Delta q_m^{sc}$ is the projected change in monthly
mean discharge under scenario condition in percent as per
\cite{wang2024historical}, and $M_s$ is the set of months that fall into season
$s\in S$, where months Feb--Apr are mapped to the Pre-Monsoon season, months May--Oct
are mapped to the Monsoon season, and Nov--Jan are mapped to the Post-Monsoon
season. 

To estimate how cost reduction benefits may change under climate scenario
conditions, we find the subset $Y^{sc}\subseteq Y$ that is closest to the
expected future climate in terms of seasonal mean discharge. Specifically, we
select the subset that minimizes the root mean squared error between
its seasonal mean discharge $\left\{{\overline{q_s^{Y'}}}\right\}_{s\in S}$ and
the expected seasonal mean discharge under climate scenario conditions
$\left\{{\overline{q_s^{sc}}}\right\}_{s\in S}$:
\begin{equation}
  Y^{sc} = \argmin{Y'\subseteq Y} \sqrt{\frac{1}{|S|}\sum_{s \in S}\left(\overline{q_s^{sc}} - \overline{q_s^{Y'}}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
with 
\begin{equation}
  \overline{q_s^{Y'}} = \frac{1}{|Y'| |M_s|} \sum_{y\in Y'} \sum_{m\in M_s} q_{m,y}.
\end{equation}
Note that the set $Y'$ in the above equations can have any cardinality between 1
and 19, as the full sample contains 19 years. The selected years for both
climate scenarios are shown in \cref{fig:hydropower_year_selection}. We use the
mean cost reduction across selected years as the resulting potential cost
reduction. 

As \cite{wang2024historical} assess projections for the future period of 2070 to
2099, we assume that these benefits can be achieved by 2085 and scale them to
the year 2050 with the projected temperature under optimistic and pessimistic
scenarios. For the optimistic climate projections, this yields potential
reduction in electricity generation cost of 8.5\% in 2050 under the status quo
scenario and 9.9\% under continued improvement. In the pessimistic climate
scenario, benefits amount to only 7.7\% and 9.1\%, respectively. Benefits over
the entire simulation period are shown in
\cref{fig:hydropower_impact_channel_KHM} and
\cref{tab:hydropower_impact_channel_KHM}. 
 
These changes in annual electricity generation cost enter the MFMod model by
affecting the disturbance term of the factor cost equation (variable $OPEX_t$ in 
\cref{eq:hydro_link_equation}, see \cref{sec:methods_mfmod_productivity}).

\subsection{Macro-economic model: MFMod for Cambodia}
\label{sec:methods_mfmod}

MFMod is a semi-structural macroeconometric model developed by the World Bank
and maintained for 181 individual countries \parencite{burns_world_2019}. It is
designed to support macroeconomic forecasting and policy simulation and has been
progressively extended to accommodate climate and disaster risk applications,
referred to as MFMod-CC \parencite{burns2021climate,burns2021macroeconomic}. The
model combines macroeconomic accounting identities with empirically estimated
behavioral equations and is widely used in World Bank country work, including
Country Climate and Development Reports (CCDRs) and macro-fiscal policy
analysis.

MFMod follows a stock-flow consistent macroeconomic framework linking the real
economy, fiscal sector, external sector, and financial conditions. Economic
activity is determined through interactions between aggregate demand, productive
capacity, labor markets, and fiscal dynamics, while maintaining consistency with
national accounts and balance of payments identities. Unlike computable general
equilibrium (CGE) models, MFMod is estimated using time-series econometric
techniques, typically employing error-correction models that capture both
short-run dynamics and long-run equilibrium relationships. This structure allows
the model to simulate the medium- to long-term macroeconomic impacts of policy
changes and structural shocks.

The model is calibrated to country-specific macroeconomic data and institutional
characteristics. For this study, a Cambodia-specific version of MFMod was
constructed using historical macroeconomic and sectoral data. The model is used
to simulate how improved hydrometeorological (hydromet) information affects
economic outcomes through three transmission channels: reduced disaster losses,
improved agricultural productivity, and enhanced hydropower generation.

\subsubsection{Core model structure}
Economic output is determined by a combination of aggregate demand and supply
capacity. On the supply side, potential output $Y^*_t$ is determined by a
standard production function combining capital $K$, labor $L$, and productivity
$A$:
\begin{equation}
  Y^*_t = A_t\,K_{t-1}^{1-\alpha}\,L_t^{\alpha}
\end{equation}
The capital stock evolves according to the standard accumulation equation:
\begin{equation}
  K_t = (1 - \delta)\,K_{t-1} + I_t
\end{equation}
where $I_t$ represents total investment and $\delta$ is the depreciation rate.

In the short run, actual output $Y_t$ may deviate from potential output due to
demand fluctuations. Aggregate demand is composed of private consumption,
investment, government spending, and net exports:
\begin{equation}
  Y_t = C_t + I_t + G_t + X_t - M_t
\end{equation}

Each component of output is estimated using time-series econometric techniques
based on error-correction models, allowing for different speeds of adjustment
toward long-run equilibrium relationships. Consumption depends on real
disposable income and wealth, while investment responds to output expectations,
capacity utilization, and financing conditions. Exports depend on global demand
and external competitiveness, whereas imports are driven by gross domestic
expenditure and relative prices. Government demand is linked to fiscal spending
decisions, while fiscal revenues and expenditures determine the fiscal balance
and the evolution of public debt.

Production prices incorporate both cost-push factors, such as changes in factor
costs, and demand-pull pressures captured by the output gap, i.e. the difference
between potential and actual output. Final demand prices follow developments in
domestic production costs as well as movements in global prices.


\subsubsection{Modelling the Benefits of Hydrometeorological Systems}
The economic benefits of hydromet investments are incorporated in MFMod through
three transmission channels: flood damage reduction, improved agricultural
productivity, and higher hydropower productivity. Shocks are introduced relative
to the baseline (Control) scenario to represent the benefits of improved
hydromet services. Two hydromet service scenarios are considered: Status Quo and
Improvement, each evaluated under two climate futures: Optimistic and
Pessimistic.

The benefits of improved hydromet services are modeled as reductions in
climate-related damages. Capital expenditures (CAPEX) associated with hydromet
systems are introduced as additional public investment financed through
government borrowing, while operational and maintenance costs (OPEX) are assumed
to be borne by final users.

Although damage functions are commonly used to estimate the economic impacts of
climate change \parencite{nordhaus1992optimal,nordhaus1992dice}, this approach
has been widely criticized for relying on highly aggregated functional forms and
limited empirical evidence, which can lead to substantial uncertainty and may
underestimate nonlinear and sector-specific climate impacts
\parencite{pindyck2013climate,stern2013structure}. Moreover, such approaches
often obscure important transmission channels and adaptation dynamics across
sectors \parencite{burke2015global}. Instead, damages in MFMod are introduced as
a vector of computed impacts derived from biophysical models
\parencite{abalo2025macroeconomic}. This approach avoids the need to estimate
aggregate damage functions statistically and allows the model to capture
distinct impact channels as well as the role of adaptation and resilience
measures.

The following sections describe how each of the three channels is represented in
the model.


\subsubsection{Modelling Disaster Risk Reduction}
\label{sec:methods_mfmod_drr}
Empirical evidence suggests that the macroeconomic impacts of disasters often
exceed what would be expected from capital destruction alone, as disruptions to
infrastructure, supply chains, and economic activity amplify output losses
\parencite{von2012unmitigated,bakkensen2018climate}. To capture these broader
effects, macroeconomic climate models increasingly represent disasters as
affecting both the stock of capital and its productivity
\parencite{hallegatte2019losses}. Following this approach, flood impacts in
MFMod are modeled through damages to productive capital and the dynamics of
reconstruction.

The modelling of capital losses follows the framework used in recent World Bank
macroeconomic climate modelling
\parencite{burns2021climate,hallegatte2024modeling}. The effective capital stock
is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  K=\left(1 -\frac{DS}{\widetilde{K}}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}\widetilde{K}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{K}$ denotes the productive capital stock prior to damages,
$DS$ represents the stock of destroyed capital due to flood events, and
$(1-\alpha)$ is the capital share in production. This formulation assumes that
destroyed capital has approximately the average productivity of capital, while
newly installed capital yields marginal productivity. Flood events increase the
stock of destroyed capital according to:
\begin{equation}
  DS_t = DS_{t-1} + RD_t - I_t^R
  \label{eq:drr_link_equation}
\end{equation}
where $RD_t$ represents residual damage (derived in \cref{sec:methods_drr}) and
$I_t^R$ denotes reconstruction investment. Although reconstruction typically
yields high economic returns, reconstruction capacity is limited by
institutional and sectoral constraints. Reconstruction investment is therefore
constrained to a fixed share of total investment:
\begin{equation}
  I_t^R = \min (DS_t, \phi\,I_t)
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ represents the maximum share of total investment that can be directed
toward reconstruction.

Hydrometeorological investments are introduced as adaptation measures that
reduce residual flood damages $RD_t$. Improved monitoring and early warning
systems allow households, firms, and governments to better prepare for flood
events, thereby reducing effective economic losses. In the model, these
investments reduce the magnitude of residual damages entering the capital
destruction process. The resulting changes in the capital stock feed into the
production function through the effective capital stock $K$, thereby affecting
potential output and overall economic activity.

\subsubsection{Modelling Sectoral Productivity (Agriculture and Hydropower)}
\label{sec:methods_mfmod_productivity}

Given the aggregate structure of MFMod and the absence of explicit production
functions for individual sectors, sectoral impacts are introduced through
changes in total factor productivity (TFP) or through changes in production
costs. 

For agriculture, improved weather forecasts influence crop production conditions
and input decisions. These effects are represented as changes in agricultural
productivity. To incorporate this channel, the potential output equation is
modified to allow agricultural productivity shocks to affect aggregate supply: 
\begin{equation}
  Y_t^*=(1+AD_t\,\omega^{Y_{AGR}})\,A_t\,K_{t-1}^{1-\alpha}L_t^\alpha
  \label{eq:agri_link_equation}
\end{equation}
where $AD_t$ represents the agricultural productivity shock associated with
improved hydrometeorological information (derived in
\cref{sec:methods_agriculture}), and $\omega^{Y_{AGR}}$ denotes the share of
agriculture in total output, which scales the sector-specific productivity
change to the aggregate economy.

Since MFMod explicitly measures capital and labor inputs but not
land, part of the agricultural productivity residual implicitly captures land
productivity and weather-related effects. Forecast-driven improvements in
agricultural conditions therefore enter the model as shocks to agricultural TFP.

For hydropower, improved hydrometeorological information enhances reservoir
management and electricity generation efficiency, resulting in lower operating
costs. In MFMod, these efficiency gains are introduced as reductions in
electricity production costs. In the model implementation, the change in
operating costs ($\Delta OPEX_t$ as derived in \cref{sec:methods_hydropower})
enters the disturbance term of the factor cost equation ($e_t^{c'}$), scaled by
the electricity share in production costs ($\omega^{elec}$), while the
underlying cost dynamics remain determined endogenously by the model.
\begin{equation}
  e_t^{c'}=e_t^c + \omega^{elec}\,\Delta OPEX_t
  \label{eq:hydro_link_equation}
\end{equation}


Through this mechanism, improved hydropower operations reduce economy-wide
energy costs, which in turn affect production costs, relative prices, and
aggregate economic activity.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

MFMod simulations indicate that improved hydrometeorological forecast services
yield persistent, and in many scenarios significant, GDP gains for Cambodia
across all three impact channels and across both climate trajectories, with
benefits growing through 2050 as forecast uptake matures and climate risks
intensify. The economic gains substantially exceed the modeled investment costs
(\cref{tab:scenario_costs_KHM}) by several orders of magnitude, providing a case
for the economic viability of sustained hydromet investment (see
\cref{sec:discussion_cost_effectiveness} for a cost-effectiveness analysis
discussion).
\Cref{fig:results_DRR_control,fig:results_AGR_control,fig:results_HYD_control,fig:results_All_control}
report GDP deviations from the respective control scenarios under each forecast
scenario. The underlying data is shown in
\cref{tab:results_DRR_control_rel,tab:results_AGR_control_rel,tab:results_HYD_control_rel,tab:results_All_control_rel}.
\cref{fig:gdp_differences} summarizes the results at the end of the
simulation horizon, i.e., 2050. In this section, we first present results for
each impact channel in isolation and then for all three channels jointly.


\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=7in]{./figures/results_gdp_differences.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Differences in GDP outcomes across hydromet services and
  climate change scenarios by 2050.} Status quo versus control denotes GDP gains
  when forecast skill is held constant, compared to the control without any
  forecast benefits. Improvement vs. control shows the potential GDP gains if
  forecast skill continues to improve, again compared to the control setup.
  Improvement vs. status quo is the increase in GDP from continued investments,
  compared to current forecast skill level benefits.}
  \label{fig:gdp_differences}

We distinguish three analytically distinct comparisons that carry
different policy meanings: (i) the status quo versus the control scenario,
quantifying the economic value of existing forecast services relative to their
complete absence; (ii) the continued improvement versus the control scenario,
representing the full potential of forecast investment; and (iii) the continued
improvement versus the status quo, isolating the marginal return to further
forecast investment beyond current service levels—the comparison most relevant
to actual investment decisions.

\paragraph{Disaster risk reduction (DRR) channel.}
When only the DRR channel is active, GDP gains are positive but moderate under
the optimistic climate scenario and substantially larger under the pessimistic
scenario (\cref{fig:results_DRR_control}, \cref{tab:results_DRR_control_rel}).
Under the optimistic scenario, GDP is 0.44\% and 0.55\% above the control by
2050 under the status quo and continued improvement forecast scenarios,
respectively. Under the pessimistic scenario, the corresponding figures rise to
0.84\% and 1.07\%. The marginal GDP gain from continued improvement over the
status quo amounts to 0.1\% under the optimistic scenario and doubles to 0.22\%
under the pessimistic scenario by 2050—a difference that grows with climate
severity, reflecting the increasing value of additional forecast lead time as
baseline flood exposure rises. 

The amplification of benefits in the pessimistic climate is
consistent with the underlying damage function: as baseline flood losses
increase from 0.93\% of national capital stock in 2020 to 2.97\% by 2050 in the
pessimistic control, avoided damages—and their macroeconomic multiplier—expand
commensurately (\cref{tab:drr_impact_channel_KHM}). The gap between the two
forecast scenarios also widens over time, reflecting the increasing value of
additional lead time and forecast quality as climate risks mount. Viewed from
the perspective of total climate exposure (\cref{fig:results_DRR_baseline}),
early warning systems with maintained or improved forecast skill partially---but
not fully---offset growing climate losses, reducing the GDP deficit relative to
the no-climate-change baseline from 19.1\% (optimistic control) to 18.4\% and
10.3\% respectively under pessimistic and optimistic improvement scenarios by
2050.

\paragraph{Agricultural productivity channel}
Agriculture generates the largest standalone GDP gains of the three channels
throughout the projection horizon (\cref{fig:results_AGR_control},
\cref{tab:results_AGR_control_rel}). GDP deviations above control increase
nearly linearly as forecast benefits are phased in, reaching 1.88\% (status quo)
and 3.06\% (continued improvement) by 2050 under the optimistic scenario. The
pessimistic scenario yields marginally lower but broadly similar gains of 1.84\%
(status quo) and 3.00\% (continued improvement). At the end of the simulation
period, the marginal GDP benefits to continued improvement over the status quo
are approximately the same under optimistic and pessimistic climate scenarios,
which are 1.16\% and 1.14\%, respectively. 

This near-symmetry across
climate scenarios reflects the structure of the agricultural input channel
(\cref{tab:agri_impact_channel_KHM}): forecast-driven total factor
productivity (TFP) gains are similar under both climate scenarios in absolute
terms (approximately +10.2 percentage points relative to the pessimistic control
and +10.2 percentage points relative to the optimistic control at 2050 under
status quo), because they are defined relative to the climate-specific control
path rather than in absolute terms. The strong GDP response to the agricultural
channel---roughly one-fifth to one-sixth of the sector-level productivity shock---is
mediated through Cambodia's substantial agricultural sector share (17\% of GDP)
and downstream effects on capital accumulation, employment, and exports.

\paragraph{Hydropower channel}
The hydropower channel contributes positive but comparatively small GDP gains in
isolation (\cref{fig:results_HYD_control}, \cref{tab:results_HYD_control_rel}).
By 2050, GDP is 0.16\% (status quo) and 0.18\% (continued improvement) above
control under the optimistic scenario, and 0.15\% and 0.16\% under the
pessimistic scenario. The marginal GDP gain from continued improvement over the
status quo is accordingly narrow: approximately 0.02\% under both optimistic and
pessimistic climate scenarios by 2050. The modest difference between status quo
and improvement in GDP terms---only 0.02 to 0.01 percentage points---mirrors the
narrow spread in electricity generation cost inputs
(\cref{tab:hydropower_impact_channel_KHM}): by 2050, status quo and improved
operations achieve 8.5\% and 9.9\% cost reductions under the optimistic
scenario, with a 1.4 percentage point spread translating into a barely
discernible GDP difference through the electricity cost channel. The slightly
lower benefits under the pessimistic climate scenario (compared with the
optimistic) are consistent with seasonally heterogeneous discharge changes under
RCP 8.5, in particular the modeled decline in dry-season discharge that reduces
the scope for cost-optimal dispatch (\cref{fig:hydropower_year_selection}).



\paragraph{Combined impact across all channels}
When all three channels are activated simultaneously, modeled GDP gains become
sizeable and grow continuously through 2050
(\cref{fig:results_All_control},
\cref{tab:results_All_control_rel}). Under the optimistic climate
scenario, GDP is 2.50\% above control by 2050 under the status quo and 3.83\%
under continued improvement. Under the pessimistic scenario, combined gains are
larger: 2.86\% (status quo) and 4.30\% (continued improvement). The larger
combined benefit under the pessimistic climate scenario---despite lower
agricultural and hydropower contributions in that scenario---reflects the
dominance of the DRR channel under high-damage conditions: greater baseline
climate exposure amplifies the macroeconomic payoff from avoided flood losses.
This interaction is a key finding of the analysis: worsening climate change
increases, not decreases, the total economic return to maintaining and improving
hydrometeorological forecast services.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=7in]{./figures/results_All_control_rel.pdf}

  from their respective control scenarios under optimistic and pessimistic
  climate projections, when all three impact channels are simulated together.
  Data in \cref{tab:results_All_control_rel}.}
  \label{fig:results_All_control}
\end{figure}

The incremental benefit of continued forecast improvement over the status quo is
substantial in the combined runs. By 2050, the total GDP benefits of forecast
improvement amount to 1.3\% in the optimistic climate scenario and 1.4\% in the
pessimistic scenario, when compared to the status quo. These differences widen
over time, indicating that the economic returns to forecast improvement compound
rather than remaining static.

\paragraph{Relative contributions and channel interactions}
Comparing the isolated channel runs with the combined run reveals the relative
importance of each pathway. At 2050 under the optimistic/status quo
configuration, the isolated GDP deviations are 0.44\% (DRR), 1.88\% (agriculture),
and 0.16\% (hydropower), summing to 2.48\%, compared with the combined effect of
2.50\%. Under the pessimistic/improvement configuration, isolated effects are
1.07\%, 3.00\%, and 0.16\%, summing to 4.23\% against a combined effect of 4.30\%. In
all four 2050 scenario pairs, the combined GDP deviation is 0.02--0.07 percentage
points larger than the sum of isolated effects. These small positive residuals
suggest modest cross-channel complementarities in MFMod---for example,
productivity gains from agriculture and DRR jointly reinforce capital
accumulation---rather than exact additivity. Across the full simulation period,
the qualitative rank order of channels is stable: agriculture dominates, DRR is
the second-largest contributor (and becomes increasingly important under
worsening climate conditions), and hydropower provides a smaller but positive
increment.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Main economic interpretation}
\label{sec:discussion_main}

The results demonstrate that improved hydrometeorological forecast services
generate persistent, economy-wide GDP benefits for Cambodia across all climate
scenarios and impact channels, with gains compounding over time as uptake
expands and climate risks intensify. Three features of the findings merit
particular emphasis.

First, the agriculture impact channel is the single largest GDP driver,
consistent with the structural importance of agriculture in Cambodia's economy
and the magnitude of the modeled TFP shock. The high sensitivity of aggregate
GDP to agricultural TFP changes reflects the sector's role not just in direct
value added, but also as an income source for rural households, a driver of
domestic consumption, and a determinant of factor allocation economy-wide. This
finding aligns with broader literature showing that weather forecast value is
relatively high in economies with large agricultural sectors and rainfed systems
\parencite{hansen_potential_2009,an-vo_framework_2021}.

Second, the DRR channel exhibits a distinctive and policy-relevant feature: its
value is positively correlated with the severity of climate change. Under the
pessimistic trajectory, in which annual average flood damages could reach 2.97\%
of the national capital stock by 2050 (compared with 1.01\% under the optimistic
trajectory), early warning benefits are substantially amplified. This means that
the economic case for investing in---or at minimum maintaining---forecast
infrastructure becomes stronger under the scenarios of higher climatic risk.
Forecast services therefore function not merely as an economic good but as a
climate adaptation instrument whose marginal value increases with exposure.

Third, the hydropower channel, while comparatively small in GDP terms, is
non-negligible in absolute terms and may carry system-level co-benefits not
captured by our analytical approach. Forecast-informed dispatch reduces reliance
on fossil backup generation and improves reservoir resilience, contributing to
energy security. The modest spread between status quo and improvement scenarios
in the hydropower channel also reflects that Cambodia's current forecast system
could already capture a meaningful share of available dispatch gains, suggesting
diminishing returns to further forecast improvement for this channel alone.


\subsection{Cost-effectiveness analysis}
\label{sec:discussion_cost_effectiveness}

The scenario costs in \cref{tab:scenario_costs_KHM} allow a preliminary assessment of
cost-effectiveness. Under the status quo scenario, annual operation and
maintenance costs are assumed at USD~0.5~million per year (2025 NPV). Under the
continued improvement scenario, total costs reach approximately USD~1.0~million
per year in OPEX plus USD~4.2~million per year in CAPEX during the 2031--2035
investment period, amounting to USD~5.2~million annually in peak years and
approximately USD~1.0~million annually thereafter. Even at the low end of
modeled GDP gains, the implied benefit-cost ratios are extremely large.

To illustrate this: Cambodia's GDP in 2024 was approximately USD~46.3~billion. A
GDP gain of 0.13\% in 2025 alone---the modeled combined gain under the
optimistic/status quo scenario---corresponds to approximately USD~60~million, or
roughly 120 times the USD~0.5~million annual OPEX in that year. By 2035,
combined gains of approximately 1.0\% of a GDP growing at Cambodia's projected
pace imply annual benefit flows exceeding USD~500~million, against cumulative
annual costs of at most USD~5.2~million in the peak CAPEX year---an annual
benefit-cost ratio exceeding 100:1. Even under highly conservative
assumptions---for example, discounting all GDP gains at 10\% and assuming only a
fraction of modeled gains materialize---the present value of benefits over the
2025--2050 horizon dwarfs the present value of costs under both the status quo
and continued improvement scenarios. 

A complementary assessment considers the marginal benefit-cost ratio of
transitioning from the status quo to the continued improvement scenario. The
incremental costs of this transition comprise an additional USD~0.5~million per
year in OPEX and USD~4.2~million per year in CAPEX during 2031--2035, averaging
approximately USD~1.5~million per year in net present value terms over the full
horizon (\cref{tab:scenario_costs_KHM}). Against this, the incremental GDP gain of 1.3 to 1.4\% by
2050---corresponding to annual benefit flows of several hundred million USD at
Cambodia's projected GDP level by the 2030s---implies a marginal BCR of a similar
order of magnitude to the aggregate ratio. This result has direct relevance for
investment decision-making: the economic case for upgrading from current to
improved forecast services is as strong, on a cost-per-unit-benefit basis, as
the case for maintaining existing services rather than allowing them to lapse.

This order-of-magnitude result is robust to the choice of discount rate and the
assumed realization rate of modeled benefits, because the asymmetry between
annual investment costs (USD~0.5--5.2~million) and economy-wide GDP multipliers
(USD hundreds of millions per year by the 2030s) is so large. A formal net
present value analysis, beyond the scope of this paper, could refine these
estimates using projected GDP paths from MFMod directly.

It is important to note, however, that both the aggregate and marginal BCR
estimates are most sensitive to the agricultural productivity parameters, which
are transferred from a Vietnamese Mekong Delta context \parencite{an-vo_framework_2021}.
Though, under a conservative 50\% downward adjustment to agricultural channel
benefits—reflecting uncertainty in parameter transferability across settings—the
combined benefit-cost ratios would remain strongly positive, with annual
benefits still exceeding investment costs by a wide margin. Readers are referred
to \cref{sec:discussion_limitations} for a detailed discussion of transferability assumptions; even
under pessimistic uptake scenarios, the asymmetry between investment costs and
economy-wide GDP multipliers is large enough to preserve the core
cost-effectiveness finding.

Furthermore, it should also be noted that the costs modeled here represent
incremental investments for Cambodia specifically and may not capture the full
fiscal burden of maintaining or improving a national meteorological and
hydrological service, which includes staffing, training, and institutional
overhead beyond the infrastructure items in \cref{tab:scenario_costs_KHM}. It
also does not include complementary sectoral investments required to realize the
forecasts benefits, such as the construction of multipurpose disaster shelters
for people to take refuge and evacuate during disaster periods, and upgrading
the irrigation infrastructure so that the agriculture sector can be more
climate-smart. Including these would raise costs but is unlikely to reverse the
sign of the cost-effectiveness assessment given the magnitude of the GDP
differentials.



\subsection{Comparison with prior literature}
\label{sec:discussion_literature}

The results of this study are broadly consistent with the prior literature on
the economic value of hydrometeorological services, while extending it in
methodologically and empirically important ways. Three reference points from the
literature are particularly relevant.

\textcite{hallegatte_cost_2012} provides the most directly comparable prior
estimate of the economic returns to hydromet investment in developing countries.
Analysing the potential benefits of upgrading all developing countries to
high-income-country service standards, \textcite{hallegatte_cost_2012} estimates
BCRs of between 4:1 and 36:1. The BCRs implied by the present study are
substantially higher when computed using the same avoided-cost logic---by the
2030s, Cambodia's annual GDP benefits exceed the annual investment costs by two
or more orders of magnitude---but the two estimates are not directly comparable.
\textcite{hallegatte_cost_2012} uses a damage-reduction framework focused on
developing countries in aggregate, while our study employs a general-equilibrium
macroeconomic model that captures economy-wide multipliers, capital accumulation
dynamics, and cross-sector linkages. These transmission mechanisms can amplify
sector-level shocks into aggregate GDP, which partly explains the larger implied
BCRs in our results.

The \textcite{world2015valuing} report \textit{Valuing Weather and Climate:
Economic Assessment of Meteorological and Hydrological Services} provides the
methodological framework within which both \textcite{hallegatte_cost_2012} and
the present study can be situated. \textcite{world2015valuing} articulates the
full spectrum of valuation approaches for meteorological and hydrological
services---from non-market valuation and economic decision modelling to
avoided-cost assessments and benefit transfer---and emphasizes the importance of
understanding the full value chain from observation infrastructure through to
end-user decision-making. The multi-channel structure of the present study is
consistent with this framework: each impact channel (DRR, agriculture,
hydropower) corresponds to a distinct user group and decision context in which
forecast information generates measurable economic value. Importantly,
\textcite{world2015valuing} also cautions that benefit estimates derived from
different valuation methods are rarely directly comparable. Our study addresses
this comparability challenge by maintaining a consistent analytical framework
across all three channels and both climate scenarios. 

\textcite{farkas2025economic} provide the most comprehensive recent synthesis of the
quantitative literature on forecast value. Several of their findings are
directly relevant to interpreting the results of the present study. First,
\textcite{farkas2025economic} find that estimated benefits span several orders of
magnitude even within specific subsectors. Scaled to the global level, the
literature implies cereal agriculture benefits of USD~0 to USD~231~billion per
year, hydropower benefits of USD~3 to USD~19.7~billion per year, and DRM
benefits of USD~90~million to USD~9.3~billion per year. The Cambodia-specific
estimates in this study are consistent with these ranges when scaled by GDP
share, though direct comparison is impeded by differences in method and scope.
Second, \textcite{farkas2025economic} find that theoretical model-based studies---such as
the present one---tend to report significantly larger benefit values than
empirical or experimental studies. This pattern suggests that our MFMod-derived
GDP gains should be interpreted as estimates of the potential economic value of
forecast services under full uptake and adequate institutional capacity, not as
predictions of realised benefits. Third, \textcite{farkas2025economic} identify a
science-to-policy gap: most policy reports on forecast value rely on
back-of-the-envelope methods sourced from earlier policy documents rather than
from peer-reviewed empirical evidence. By grounding the three impact channels in
peer-reviewed sector studies and channeling them through a formal macroeconomic
model, the present study represents a methodological step toward closing this
gap.

This study extends the literature in several ways. First, by channeling forecast
benefits explicitly through a macroeconomic model (MFMod), it captures
general-equilibrium transmission mechanisms---capital accumulation, sectoral
adjustment, consumption smoothing---that sector-level partial-equilibrium analyses
omit. Second, by considering multiple impact pathways simultaneously, it
quantifies the degree to which benefits across agriculture, disaster risk, and
energy are approximately additive, a question rarely addressed in the
literature. Third, and most distinctively, it evaluates how the economic returns
to forecasting change under contrasting climate trajectories, finding that
worsening climate amplifies aggregate benefits through the DRR channel, an
interaction that has not been prominently documented in prior work.


\subsection{Limitations and uncertainty}
\label{sec:discussion_limitations}

Several limitations of the analysis should inform the interpretation of results.
First, the MFMod framework is a reduced-form macro-fiscal model calibrated to
historical relationships between sectoral shocks and aggregate GDP. While this
enables consistent, cross-scenario comparisons, it does not explicitly resolve
the microeconomic or behavioral mechanisms through which forecast information
translates into farmer decisions, power dispatch choices, or disaster
preparedness actions. The linearity assumptions embedded in the model may
overstate (or understate) aggregate responses at extremes of the damage
distribution.

Second, the agricultural productivity shocks are drawn from \textcite{an-vo_framework_2021}
and calibrated for the Vietnamese Mekong Delta context, then applied to
Cambodia. While the two settings share important agro-climatic characteristics,
the responsiveness of farmers and productivity responses to forecast information may differ due to differences in
cropping systems, irrigation infrastructure, extension service capacity, and
market integration. The modeled TFP increase of 10.34\% under current forecasts and
16.86\% under perfect forecasts represents the potential benefit and does not
account for the time, effort, and institutional investment required to translate
forecast information into farm-level decisions. The assumed linear phase-in of
agricultural benefits to 100\% by 2050 is a simplification and may overstate the
pace of uptake in contexts with limited forecast dissemination infrastructure.

Third, the hydropower benefit projections are based on simulation results from
\textcite{koh_evaluating_2024} using historical discharge data (2000--2018) and
projected climate-driven discharge changes from \textcite{wang2024historical}.
The mapping of future discharge changes onto current operational benefits rests
on an analog-year selection approach whose explanatory power varies by season:
$R^2 = 0.59$ in the monsoon season but only $0.28$ and $0.10$ in pre- and post-monsoon
periods (\cref{fig:hydropower_year_selection}). The uncertainty in dry-season
discharge projections is particularly consequential, as dry-season water
scarcity is the main constraint on hydropower generation. The results for the
hydropower channel should therefore be treated as indicative of direction and
order of magnitude rather than precise estimates.

Fourth, forecast skill is treated as independent of climate change throughout
the analysis. In practice, shifts in weather patterns and increased atmospheric
variability under higher-emissions scenarios may reduce forecast skill or
require new investment to maintain equivalent predictive performance. Ignoring
this feedback potentially overstates the benefits of both the status quo and
continued improvement scenarios.

Finally, the analysis does not model distributional effects. GDP gains are
reported in aggregate terms, but the incidence of benefits---whether they accrue
primarily to smallholder farmers, energy consumers, or capital owners---has
important equity implications that are not captured by the macroeconomic
aggregates presented here.


\subsection{Policy implications}
\label{sec:discussion_policy}

The findings carry several implications for investment policy in Cambodia and
comparable lower-middle-income countries. First, the results provide
quantitative support for sustained investment in hydrometeorological
infrastructure. The comparison of the status quo with the control scenarios
uncovers the potential existing benefits, while the comparison of the forecast
improvement with the status quo scenarios underscores the benefits of
continuously improving forecast infrastructure and services. The very high
benefit-cost ratios implied by the simulations suggest that even if realized
gains are a small fraction of the modeled potential, the investment is likely to
be economically justified. The continuation of the World Meteorological
Organization's Southeast Asia 2.0 initiative \parencite{crews2025sea2_project},
budgeted at USD~7.8~million for Cambodia and Lao PDR over four years, falls
squarely within the investment envelope assumed here.

Second, the results emphasize the importance of institutional as well as
technical capacity. The agricultural channel---the dominant GDP driver---cannot be
activated by forecast infrastructure alone; it requires last-mile dissemination
systems, agrometeorological extension services, and farm-level capacity to
translate forecast information into actionable planting, irrigation, and
harvesting decisions. Investment strategies that focus on observational and
forecast infrastructure without corresponding attention to the agricultural
advisory chain will realize only a fraction of the modeled potential.

Third, the finding that DRR benefits grow as climate risk intensifies implies
that the economic case for hydromet investment is likely to strengthen over
time, particularly under higher-emission scenarios. Policymakers considering
whether to upgrade versus merely maintain forecast infrastructure should factor
in this positive relationship between climate risk and forecast value: under
pessimistic climate trajectories, the gains from moving from status quo to
continued improvement are 1.44 percentage points of GDP by 2050---a difference
that, in absolute terms, will grow with Cambodia's economy.

Fourth, hydropower forecast benefits, though modest in GDP terms, may take on
greater significance as Cambodia's electricity system evolves. As hydropower
is likely to remain a central pillar of Cambodia's generation mix \parencite{IES2024CambodiaPDP},
forecast-informed dispatch could reduce the frequency and cost of fossil-fuel
backup activation, contributing to both fiscal savings and emissions reduction
objectives---co-benefits not captured in the GDP-centric framework used here.

Fifth, Cambodia's forecast benefits are partly contingent on access to
transboundary hydrological data from upstream riparian states. The country’s
structural vulnerability to upstream variability—in reservoir releases,
irrigation abstractions, and land-use change across the Mekong basin—may
altogether limit the predictive horizon and accuracy of local hydrological
forecasts. Strengthening Cambodia's engagement with the Mekong River Commission
(MRC) data-sharing protocols, and specifically improving real-time access to
upstream gauge and reservoir operation data, is therefore a complementary
investment to local hydromet infrastructure. Formalizing such access through MRC
agreements would be particularly relevant for the hydropower and DRR channels,
where upstream discharge conditions are a primary determinant of forecast skill.

Sixth, the benefits estimated here are public goods in nature. They are not
easily transferable for cost recovery of hydromet infrastructure and services
investments, operations, and maintenance. Consequently, the long-run fiscal
sustainability of Cambodia's national meteorological and hydrological service
requires developing a regulatory framework for private sector access to hydromet
data. The current analysis treats private actors—farmers, energy producers,
agribusinesses—primarily as beneficiaries absorbing operational costs through
user fees. The country could, for instance, establish a tiered data-access model
that distinguishes safety-critical public information, issued freely to all
users, from value-added commercial data products subject to licensing
arrangements. Tiered pricing business models of this kind are increasingly
common in comparable middle-income country contexts and could generate a stable
commercial revenue stream that reduces the national meteorological service’s
dependence on public financing over time, while preserving universal access to
critical early warning products.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
This study estimates the macroeconomic benefits of improved hydrometeorological
forecast services in Cambodia using the World Bank's MFMod macro-fiscal model.
We consider three impact channels---disaster risk reduction, agricultural
productivity, and hydropower generation---under optimistic and pessimistic climate
scenarios and three forecast scenarios (control, status quo, and continued
improvement), spanning the period 2020--2050.

The central finding is that forecast services generate substantial and growing
GDP gains under all scenarios. When all three channels operate jointly, GDP is
estimated to be 2.50--4.30\% above the no-forecast control by 2050, depending on
the forecast and climate scenario. Existing forecast infrastructure alone
(''status quo'') could already generate GDP gains of 2.50--2.86\% above the
no-forecast control baseline by 2050. Continued investment in forecast
improvement yields an additional 1.3 to 1.4\% GDP gain by 2050—a marginal return
that is economically substantial in absolute terms and implies significantly
positive benefit-cost ratios. Across both comparison pairs, benefits grow over
time as climate risks intensify and forecast uptake matures.

The agricultural productivity channel
accounts for the majority of these gains, while disaster risk reduction becomes
increasingly important---and eventually dominant over the other channels---as
climate conditions worsen. Hydropower contributes positively in all scenarios
but at a lower order of magnitude. A key result that differentiates this
analysis from prior work is that the aggregate economic return to forecast
investment is larger, not smaller, under the pessimistic climate scenario,
because the DRR channel amplifies with increasing flood damage exposure. This
implies that hydrometeorological investments function as a form of climate
adaptation whose returns grow with climate risk.

The implied benefit-cost ratios are extremely large. Even under conservative
assumptions, the present value of GDP gains over the simulation horizon exceeds
the present value of modeled investment costs by several orders of magnitude.
Annual benefit flows in the 2030s---on the order of hundreds of millions of USD~at
Cambodia's projected GDP level---dwarf the annual OPEX and CAPEX costs of USD
0.5--5.2~million. This does not make the investment trivial to implement:
translating the modeled potential into realized gains requires sustained
institutional investment, evacuation infrastructure for flood episodes,
last-mile dissemination infrastructure for agriculture, and governance
frameworks for forecast-informed hydropower dispatch. But the asymmetry between
costs and benefits is large enough to be robust to substantial implementation
shortfalls.

Several limitations temper these conclusions. The agricultural benefit
parameters are calibrated from a related but distinct regional context
(Vietnam's Mekong Delta), the linear uptake assumptions may overstate the pace
of institutional adoption, the hydropower projections carry seasonal
uncertainty, and distributional effects are not addressed. Future work should
refine sector-level transmission mechanisms, extend the analysis to other
climate-vulnerable sectors (e.g., fisheries, infrastructure maintenance), and
assess the distributional incidence of forecast benefits across income groups
and regions within Cambodia.

More broadly, the findings support the case for treating hydrometeorological
services as a high-return public investment in climate-vulnerable developing
economies. The combination of large benefit multipliers, increasing returns
under worsening climate conditions, and the cross-sector reach of forecast
information suggests that national meteorological and hydrological services are
among the most cost-effective climate adaptation investments available to
governments in the region.
